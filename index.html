The next step for Dad's brain stimulation.

His DBS device helped for years. Lately the progress has stalled — symptoms creeping back, settings that don't hold the way they used to. That's a known pattern, and there's now a newer way of running the same implanted hardware that's worth looking into. This page lays out the research in plain terms, with the details a step away whenever you want them.

01 — The situation

Good devices can stop keeping up.

Traditional DBS sends a constant, unchanging pulse into the brain, day and night. It doesn't know whether the medication is working, whether it's the middle of the night, or whether symptoms are flaring. It just runs at one setting.

Over several years, two things happen. The brain tissue can grow tolerant to a constant signal (called habituation), so the same setting does less — pushing doctors to keep turning it up, which can bring on side effects like slurred speech or balance trouble. And because the pulse never adjusts, it's often too strong when medicated and too weak when it wears off.

This is exactly the picture of "it used to work better." It doesn't necessarily mean the disease has jumped ahead — it can mean the delivery method has reached its limits.

02 — The idea

Adaptive DBS listens, then adjusts.

Adaptive DBS (aDBS) uses the same implanted leads, but turns the device into a two-way system: it reads the brain's own electrical signals and raises or lowers stimulation in real time — more when symptoms emerge, less when they ease. Just the right amount, moment to moment.

For anyone whose implant is in Medtronic's recent "Percept" family, this can often be switched on without new surgery — it's a software activation plus a careful tuning process.

Traditional (constant)

  • One fixed setting, 24 hours a day
  • Can't follow medication ups and downs
  • Brain may grow tolerant over years
  • Settings climb; side effects creep in

Adaptive (responsive)

  • Adjusts second-by-second to brain signals
  • Eases off when medicated, ramps up when needed
  • Built-in "rest" periods that fight tolerance
  • More steady relief, often longer battery life

The "how it actually knows" — the brain signals, the control logic — is in the science section if you want it. You don't need it to understand the decision.

03 — The evidence

It's FDA-approved, and patients chose to keep it.

This isn't experimental anymore. The FDA approved the adaptive system in 2025, based on a major trial called ADAPT-PD that studied 68 people much like Dad — already implanted for a few years, doing okay but not great on the old mode.

98%chose to stay on adaptive DBS rather than switch back
+1.3 hrsmore good "on" time per day, with less dyskinesia
−1.6 hrsless "off" time — the stiff, frozen, immobile stretches

A real-world follow-up study (ADAPT-START) saw patients walking notably better, with a roughly 40% drop in freezing-of-gait — one of the hardest symptoms to treat. The full numbers, including the honest caveats, are in the drilldown below.

The trial results in full

ADAPT-PD The pivotal trial — multicenter, single-blind crossover, comparing adaptive vs. the patient's own optimized continuous DBS.

  • 95% of patients on the preferred "dual-threshold" mode met the success bar; 87% on the simpler single-threshold mode.
  • The dual-threshold mode added 1.3 hours/day of good "on" time and cut 1.6 hours of "off" time — both clinically meaningful.
  • After 30 days, 98% preferred adaptive, citing fewer symptom swings (66%), better movement (63%), and fewer side effects (45%).
  • About 30% of adverse events were tied to the adaptive setup itself — almost all minor, temporary, and fixed with small reprogramming during the tuning phase.

ADAPT-START A real-world study of switching stable patients over during ordinary clinic visits.

  • Roughly 35% greater motor improvement over already-optimized old settings at one month.
  • ~40% reduction in freezing-of-gait scores.
  • The honest caveat: overall quality-of-life scores actually dipped ~28% on average. Researchers think that as people moved more and re-engaged with life, they became more aware of remaining non-movement issues (fatigue, speech). Worth discussing with a doctor, not hiding.
  • Not everyone qualifies: a clear brain signal is needed. In one real-world group, only 9 of 20 patients offered the switch were eligible — usually due to signal quality. This is a key question for any consult.

04 — Go deeper

The science, a tap away.

How the device "reads" the brain

The implant senses local field potentials — tiny voltage rhythms from groups of brain cells near the electrodes. In Parkinson's, a particular rhythm called the beta band (13–35 Hz) rises when symptoms like stiffness and slowness take over, and falls when medication or stimulation is working.

By watching that beta signal, the device gets a live readout of how Dad is doing and adjusts accordingly. Other rhythms map to other things — gamma to dyskinesia and movement, theta/alpha to tremor — and future versions will likely track several at once.

The two adjustment modes (single vs. dual threshold)

Single-threshold: one line in the sand. Cross it and stimulation jumps within milliseconds. Very fast, but jumpier — and in the trial only modestly helpful, with slightly worse battery life.

Dual-threshold (the preferred mode): an upper and lower line with a calm "leave it alone" zone in between. It eases up and down gradually over minutes, matching the slow rhythm of medication wearing in and off. This is the mode that delivered the strong results above, plus about 5% better battery life per year.

For most long-term patients, dual-threshold is becoming the gold standard.

Why this fights "tolerance" (habituation)

A constant pulse gives the brain nothing to adapt around, so over time it pushes back and the therapy fades — the reason settings keep climbing on old DBS.

Adaptive DBS lowers stimulation whenever symptoms are quiet, giving the tissue regular periods of rest. Early long-term data suggests the underlying brain signals stay stable this way, and patients hold their gains without the constant upward dose-chasing. In effect, it can act as a non-surgical "reset."

What the switch-over actually involves

It's more involved than a normal tune-up — worth knowing so it's not a surprise:

  • ~4 visits on average to dial in the settings.
  • One step requires being seen off medication (uncomfortable but brief) to find the lower limit safely.
  • 3–5 days of at-home recording so the team can match the device's readings to Dad's real daily life, then fine-tune.

This is exactly why a center's experience — and remote-programming options — matter so much.

06 — At the appointment

What to ask them.

A short list to bring to any consult.

  1. Have you switched patients from traditional to adaptive DBS before? How many, and how did they do?
  2. Is Dad's implant compatible, and does he have a clear enough brain signal to qualify? (Not everyone does.)
  3. Is the stalled progress likely device tolerance, or disease progression? How do you tell them apart?
  4. What does the programming process look like — how many visits, over what timeline?
  5. Do you offer any remote programming from home?
  6. Who makes the programming decisions — one neurologist, or a full team?
  7. How do you watch for cognitive or mood side effects as settings change?
  8. What does follow-up look like once the switch is done?
  9. Is my insurance plan in-network — and are both the surgeon and the hospital facility covered?
  10. Will I need a physician referral before being seen? (Some programs require one.)
